Becoming Friends with Your Camera

Exploring Perspective, Focal Length, and the Center of Projection

Due: Tuesday, September 2nd, 2025 at 11:59PM

Overview

In this class, we want you to become friends with every pixel. The first step toward this goal is to become friends with your cameraâ€”e.g., the one in your smartphone. In particular, the goal of this project is to get you some intuitive understanding of the somewhat subtle relationship between perspective, focal length/zoom, and the center of projection.

For this project, I explored these concepts through three different photography exercises, each demonstrating unique aspects of how camera positioning and zoom affect the final image.

Part 1: Selfie - The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Close-up (The "Wrong" Way)
Taken from close range without zoom - notice the distortion
Stepped Back + Zoomed (The "Right" Way)
Taken from several feet away with zoom to match face size (3.0 Zoom)

Reflection

The close-up photo exhibits noticeable distortion because the camera's proximity to the subject exaggerates perspective, making facial features closer to the lens (like the nose and the shape of my face) appear disproportionately large. When stepping back and using zoom, the camera captures a more flattering portrait with natural proportions, as the perspective compression is reduced (face is less oval-shaped, smaller nose size, etc.). This demonstrates how distance affects perspective distortion even when the subject appears the same size in both photos.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Zoomed In (Compressed Perspective)
Taken with zoom from a distance - notice how elements appear closer together (3.0 Zoom)
Normal View (Wide Angle)
Taken without zoom from closer position - shows more depth

Reflection

The zoomed-in photo creates a compressed perspective where elements in the scene appear closer together than they actually are. This happens because the telephoto lens (or zoom) has a narrower field of view and flattens the spatial relationship between objects at different distances. The wide-angle shot, taken from a closer position, exaggerates perspective and makes objects (e.g the trees and pathway) at different distances appear farther apart. This illustrates how focal length affects the perception of depth and space in architectural photography.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Sequence of photos of the 4.0 Ball moving back while zooming in - the "Vertigo effect"

Reflection

The dolly zoom creates a disorienting effect where the subject remains roughly the same size in the frame, but the background appears to change perspective. As I moved the camera backward while simultaneously zooming in, the field of view narrowed while maintaining the subject size (the 4.0 Ball). This creates an unsettling sensation because our visual system expects the background to change in a particular way when we move. When these expectations are violated (by changing the focal length during movement), it creates the distinctive vertigo effect that makes this technique so powerful in cinema.
